SYMPTOMS

1. Chronic pain, stiffness, tenderness, redness, and swelling in one or more joints, usually in a symmetric pattern (both hands or both feet).
2. Inflammation and pain in one or more joints, small joints of the hands, wrists, and feet.
3. Inflammation of the synovial membrane. Joints become swollen, tender, and warm, and stiffness limits their movement. With time, multiple joints are affected (polyarthritis). Most commonly involved are the small joints of the hands, feet, and cervical spine.
4. RA may also affect other parts of the body, including skin, eyes, lungs, heart, nerves, and blood. This may result in a low red blood cell count, inflammation around the lungs, and inflammation around the heart.
a. Heart: Atherosclerosis, myocardial infarction, stroke.
b. Spine: Myelopathy.
c. Spinal Cord: Due to erosion of the odontoid process and/or transverse ligaments in the cervical spine's connection to the skull, atlanto-axial subluxation can occur. Such erosion (>3mm) can give rise to vertebrae slipping over one another and can lead to compression of the spinal cord.
d. Joints: Local osteoporosis around inflammed joints.
e. Tooth: Periodontitis (chronic infection that affects the gums and tissues supporting teeth) and thus may also lead to tooth loss.
f. Eyes: Dry eyes and mouth.
5. Joint stiffness lasts longer than 30 minutes (typically after waking in the morning or after resting for a long period of time).
6. In the early stages, people with RA may not see redness or swelling in the joints, but they may experience tenderness and pain. Having a lot of inflammation and other symptoms is called a 'flare'. A flare can last for days or months.
7. Sometimes, RA symptoms may worsen in “flares” due to stress, environmental factors (such as cigarette smoke or viral infections), too much activity, or suddenly stopping medications.
