Frequently Asked Questions

Can RA be cured?

No, RA cannot be cured, but it can be managed and controlled to slow its progression.

Do we have to continue the medications rest of our lives?

Yes, in the majority of cases, the patients must keep taking background DMARD treatment to prevent swelling of the joints and avoid damage. The withdrawal of this treatment almost always leads to a flare-up.

I have been told Iâ€™m negative for rheumatoid arthritis antibodies. Can I still have rheumatoid arthritis?

Yes, between 10% and 25% of patients do not present the typical antibodies for rheumatoid arthritis (RF and ACPA), but still have symptoms and signs that are suggestive of the disease. This is referred to as seronegative arthritis.

What are joint injections for?

Glucocorticoid joint injections are very useful for treating flare-ups in a single joint and avoid changes having to be made to the background treatment.

Why do joints make popping and cracking noises?

This noise is due to the roughness of the joint surface due to loss of the smooth cartilage.

What are the other types of arthritis?

1. Osteoarthritis (a degenerative joint disease characterized by the breakdown of cartilage, leading to pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility, often affecting weight-bearing joints).
2. Fibromyalgia (It is a chronic condition marked by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and tenderness in localized areas, often accompanied by sleep disturbances and cognitive issues).
3. Rheumatoid arthritis (An autoimmune disorder that causes chronic inflammation of the joints, leading to pain, swelling, and potential joint deformity, often affecting multiple joints symmetrically).
4. Gout (A form of arthritis caused by the accumulation of uric acid crystals in the joints, leading to sudden, severe pain, redness, and swelling, often affecting the big toe).
5. Low back pain (A common condition characterized by discomfort or pain in the lower back, which can result from various causes, including muscle strain, herniated discs, or underlying medical conditions).
6. Bursitis and tendinitis (Inflammation of the bursa (bursitis) or tendons (tendinitis) usually caused by repetitive motion or overuse, resulting in pain, swelling, and reduced range of motion in the affected areas).
7. Ankylosing spondylitis (A chronic inflammatory disease primarily affecting the spine and sacroiliac joints, leading to pain and stiffness, and potentially causing fusion of the vertebrae over time).
8. Juvenile rheumatoid arthritis (An autoimmune disorder affecting children, characterized by persistent joint inflammation, pain, and potential growth issues or joint damage).
9. Polymyalgia rheumatica (An inflammatory condition that causes muscle pain and stiffness, particularly in the shoulders and hips, often associated with age and may respond well to corticosteroids).
10. Lupus erythematosus (A complex autoimmune disease that can affect multiple organ systems, causing symptoms like joint pain, fatigue, skin rashes, and immune system dysfunction).
