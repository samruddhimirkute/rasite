DIAGNOSIS

Rheumatoid nodules are lumps that can sometimes appear on the skin and are the most common feature of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) outside of the joints.
About 30% of people with RA develop these nodules, which can range in size from a few millimeters to a few centimeters.
Typical location of these lumps are in the areas that get a lot of pressure, like the elbows, heels, or knuckles. Usually people who test positive for RA shows the presence of such lumps. Thus, these lumps can acts as markers for indicating the presence of RA.

Laboratory Tests

These tests are positive approximately two-thirds of the time, but a negative RF or CCP antibody does not rule out RA; rather, the arthritis is called seronegative, which occurs in approximately a third of people with RA. During the first year of illness, rheumatoid factor is more likely to be negative with some individuals becoming seropositive.

• Rheumatoid factor (RF) :
Rheumatoid Factor (RF) testing is based on the detection of auto antibodies in the blood. RF antibodies are typically IgM antibodies that target the Fc region of IgG antibodies. If RF is present, it binds to IgG in the test. This indicating a positive result.
• Anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide antibody (anti-CCP) :
Anti – CCP detects antibodies that target specific citrullinated proteins. Here, synthetic cyclic citrullinated peptides are coated onto a plate (These peptides are structurally similar to those found in joint tissue affected by RA). If anti-CCP antibodies are present in the patient’s serum, they will bind to the citrullinated peptides. A secondary antibody which is linked to an enzyme, is used. This secondary antibody binds to the patient’s anti-CCP antibodies. A substrate is used which lead to a measurable signal (color change) if anti-CCP is present. A positive result indicates a higher likelihood of rheumatoid arthritis.
• Complete blood count :
This test measures RBCs, WBCs, Plateletes, Hemoglobin count and Hematocrit. Based on the results of this tests, the results are interpreted.
Increased WBC count and increased platelets typicaly leads to inflammations.
• Erythrocyte sedimentation rate ( ESR ):
It measures the rate at which red blood cells settle at the bottom of a test tube over a specific period. In the presence of inflammation, certain proteins (like fibrinogen) increase in the blood, causing RBCs to clump together and settle down faster.
• C-reactive protein (CRP):
CRP is produced by the liver in response to inflammation. This test measures the level of CRP in the blood. CRP level rises quickly in response to inflammation within hours.

Imaging Tests

• X-rays :
In the early phases of RA, X-rays may appear normal or show minor changes, such as soft tissue swelling.
As the disease progresses, X-rays can show bone erosions around joints, joint space narrowing, periarticular osteopenia (decreased bone density near the joint).
• Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and ultrasound :
MRI can identify early signs of joint damage, such as bone edema and synovitis.
Ultrasound allows for real-time imaging, enabling the examination of joint movement and fluid dynamics .It can guide injections directly into joints or cysts.
• Computed tomography (CT) scanning
• Positron emission tomography (PET) scan.
• Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA).
